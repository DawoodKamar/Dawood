1. Create `src/content.config.ts` with this exact content:
   ```typescript
   import { defineCollection } from "astro:content";
   import { glob } from "astro/loaders";
   import { z } from "astro/zod";

   const blog = defineCollection({
     loader: glob({ pattern: "**/*.{md,mdx}", base: "./src/content/blog" }),
     schema: z.object({
       title: z.string(),
       description: z.string(),
       date: z.coerce.date(),
       category: z.enum([
         "AI & Work",
         "Focus & Discipline",
         "Culture & Place",
         "Clarity & Communication",
       ]),
       tags: z.array(z.string()).default([]),
       draft: z.boolean().default(false),
     }),
   });

   export const collections = { blog };
   ```

   ```markdown
   ---
   title: "Most People Aren't Behind on AI — They're Overloaded by Noise"
   description: "The real problem isn't that people aren't using AI tools. It's that the signal-to-noise ratio in AI discourse is terrible."
   date: 2026-03-15
   category: "AI & Work"
   tags: ["ai", "productivity", "signal-noise"]
   draft: false
   ---

   Every week, a new AI tool launches with breathless promises. Every week, your LinkedIn feed fills with people who've apparently revolutionized their workflow overnight. And every week, you wonder if you're falling behind.

   You're not.

   ## The Real Problem

   The issue isn't that people aren't adopting AI fast enough. The issue is that the conversation around AI has become almost entirely noise. Influencers racing to post the hottest take. Founders wrapping thin GPT wrappers in startup jargon. Consultants selling transformation roadmaps for tools that didn't exist last Tuesday.

   Meanwhile, the people actually doing meaningful work with AI are quiet. They're not posting. They're building.

   ## Signal vs. Noise

   Here's what I've noticed working at the intersection of AI and enterprise: the most impactful uses of AI are boring. They're not flashy demos or viral Twitter threads. They're things like:

   - Automating a three-hour weekly data reconciliation into a fifteen-minute review
   - Using language models to draft first-pass documentation that a human then refines
   - Building internal tools that surface the right information at the right time

   None of these make for good LinkedIn content. All of them create genuine value.

   ## What Actually Matters

   If you're feeling overwhelmed by AI, here's my honest advice: ignore ninety percent of what you see online. Instead, focus on three things:

   1. **Identify your bottlenecks.** Where do you spend time on repetitive, low-judgment work? That's where AI helps most.
   2. **Learn one tool deeply.** Pick the one most relevant to your work and actually learn it. Don't skim five tools and master none.
   3. **Talk to practitioners, not pundits.** The people using AI daily have better insights than the people tweeting about it daily.

   ## The Competitive Advantage

   The real competitive advantage isn't being first to adopt every new tool. It's being thoughtful about which tools actually solve your problems. It's having the discipline to ignore the noise and focus on what moves the needle.

   Most people aren't behind on AI. They're just overwhelmed by a firehose of content designed to make them feel that way. Turn down the volume. Focus on the signal.
   ```

4. Create `src/content/blog/focus-advantage.md` with this content (300-500 words):
   ```markdown
   ---
   title: "Focus Is Becoming a Competitive Advantage"
   description: "In a world of constant distraction, the ability to concentrate deeply is increasingly rare — and increasingly valuable."
   date: 2026-03-22
   category: "Focus & Discipline"
   tags: ["focus", "deep-work", "productivity"]
   draft: false
   ---

   I used to think productivity was about doing more things. Now I think it's about doing fewer things, better.

   This shift didn't happen overnight. It came from years of watching talented people — smart, capable, well-resourced people — underperform because they couldn't sit still long enough to finish anything meaningful.

   ## The Distraction Economy

   We live in an economy that profits from your attention being fractured. Every app on your phone is engineered to pull you away from whatever you're doing. Every notification is a small interruption that carries a large cognitive cost.

   Cal Newport wasn't exaggerating when he called deep work rare and valuable. If anything, he was understating it. The ability to focus for sustained periods isn't just a nice-to-have skill anymore. It's becoming the dividing line between people who ship and people who scroll.

   ## What Focus Actually Looks Like

   Focus isn't about willpower. It's about systems. The people I know who consistently do deep work have built environments that make focus the default:

   - They batch communications into specific time windows
   - They work in physical or digital spaces stripped of distractions
   - They protect their mornings for their most important work
   - They say no to meetings that could be emails

   This isn't glamorous. It's not a productivity hack you can implement in five minutes. It's a daily practice that compounds over months and years.

   ## The Compound Effect

   Here's what most people miss about focus: its returns are exponential, not linear. An hour of deep focus doesn't produce twice the output of thirty minutes. It produces ten times the output, because the first thirty minutes are often just getting into the problem.

   When you consistently protect time for deep work, you start to see results that seem disproportionate to the time invested. A complex project that should take two weeks gets done in one. A problem that's been lingering for months suddenly has a clear solution.

   ## The Uncomfortable Truth

   The uncomfortable truth is that most of us know what we should be working on. We just don't want to sit with the discomfort of actually doing it. Checking email feels productive. Scrolling Twitter feels like research. Attending meetings feels like collaboration.

   But none of it moves the needle the way two hours of uninterrupted focus on your most important problem does.

   Focus is becoming a competitive advantage because so few people are willing to practice it. The question isn't whether you can afford to focus. It's whether you can afford not to.
   ```

   date: 2026-03-29
   category: "Culture & Place"
   tags: ["switzerland", "ambition", "culture"]

   ---

   When I first moved to Switzerland, I expected efficiency. I expected punctuality. I expected chocolate and mountains and trains that run on time.

   What I didn't expect was a fundamentally different relationship with ambition.

   ## The Quiet Ambition

   In many cultures, ambition is loud. It's about growth at all costs, disruption as a virtue, moving fast and breaking things. Success is measured in scale — how big, how fast, how much.

   Switzerland operates differently. Here, ambition is quiet. It's about doing one thing exceptionally well, for a very long time. It's the watchmaker who's been perfecting the same mechanism for forty years. The chocolate maker whose recipe hasn't changed in a century — because it doesn't need to.

   This isn't complacency. It's a different theory of value. One that says: if you do something with enough care and precision, the world will find its way to your door.

   ## Craft Over Scale

   The Swiss model prioritizes craft over scale. A company with fifty employees that's been profitable for three generations is respected here in a way that a rapidly scaling startup burning through venture capital simply isn't.

   This took me a while to understand. Coming from a culture that celebrates unicorns and billion-dollar valuations, the idea that staying small could be a deliberate strategy — not a failure of ambition — was genuinely novel.

   ## What I've Learned

   Living here has changed how I think about my own work. I've become less interested in doing more and more interested in doing better. Less focused on reach and more focused on depth.

   Some specific shifts:

   - I value sustainability over speed. Building something that lasts matters more than building something fast.
   - I've learned to appreciate iteration. The first version doesn't need to be perfect. But the tenth version should be excellent.
   - I've become more comfortable with patience. Good work takes time. Rushing it doesn't make it better — it just makes it done.

   ## The Long Game

   Switzerland has taught me that the long game is underrated. In a world obsessed with quarterly results and viral moments, there's something radical about committing to a craft for decades.

   The mountains here have been around for millions of years. They're not going anywhere. There's a lesson in that, if you're willing to sit with it long enough to hear it.

   This is the kind of ambition I want to practice: patient, precise, and built to last.
